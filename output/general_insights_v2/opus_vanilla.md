# Opus Vanilla

Input: How does a todo app's interface reshape the user's cognition about work itself? What does making 'priority' a first-class feature teach users to believe about their own productivity? What cognitive distortions does the tool create that the user can never see from inside the tool?

---

This is a great question that operates at exactly the level where tools become invisible to their users. Let me work through it.

## The todo app as cognitive prosthesis

A todo app doesn't record your work. It **replaces your relationship to work** with its own ontology, then hides the replacement.

### What "priority" teaches you to believe

The moment a tool gives you a priority field — High/Medium/Low, or 1-5, or colored flags — it encodes a specific claim: **that importance is a static, ordinal property of tasks.**

This is false in at least three ways:

1. **Importance is relational, not intrinsic.** A task's priority depends on what else exists, what changed today, who's waiting. But the field sits there, unchanging, until you manually update it. The tool treats priority as a fact about the task. You start to as well.

2. **Importance is not ordinal.** Real work has incommensurable dimensions — urgency, impact, learning value, political necessity, emotional cost. Flattening these into a single axis trains you to believe trade-offs don't exist. You stop noticing that the "low priority" task is the one that would restructure how you think about all the others.

3. **The act of assigning priority becomes a substitute for doing the work.** You feel productive organizing. The tool rewards categorization with the same dopamine as completion. Grooming the backlog feels like progress because the tool can't distinguish between the two.

### Cognitive distortions the user can never see from inside

**The Completability Illusion.** A todo list implies that work is a finite set of discrete items that can be "done." But most meaningful work is continuous, open-ended, and generates more work as you do it. The tool can only represent the kind of work that has an end state. So you unconsciously redefine "work" to mean "things that can be checked off" — and the continuous, generative, ambiguous work (relationships, learning, strategy) disappears from your model of what you should be doing.

**The Visibility Bias.** What's on the list exists. What's not on the list doesn't. The tool cannot represent "things you haven't thought of yet" or "things you're avoiding so successfully you don't know you're avoiding them." The list becomes a census of your obligations, and you trust it the way you'd trust a map — forgetting that the map was drawn by you, with all your current blind spots intact.

**The Granularity Trap.** The tool rewards decomposition. Break big things into small things. But this teaches you that all work is decomposable — that understanding is just a matter of sufficient granularity. Some work resists decomposition: the task "figure out what's actually wrong with this project" cannot be broken into subtasks without already having the answer. The tool has no representation for "sit with ambiguity," so you learn to avoid ambiguity entirely.

**The Recency-Completion Cycle.** Old undone items create guilt. The tool surfaces them, silently. You either do them (regardless of current relevance), delete them (and feel failure), or stop opening the app. The tool frames persistence as virtue and abandonment as failure, when often the most productive thing is to let a task die without ceremony.

### The invisible distortion

The deepest one: **the tool teaches you that productivity is a property of you, measurable by your throughput of discrete items.** Not a property of your context, your relationships, your clarity of thought, or your ability to notice what matters.

You can never see this from inside the tool because the tool IS the measuring instrument. Asking "am I productive?" while looking at your todo app is like asking "is this room bright?" while wearing tinted glasses. The answer is always in the tool's terms, and the tool's terms are the distortion.

The todo app doesn't help you do your work. It helps you do *its* version of your work — and then makes the difference invisible.
